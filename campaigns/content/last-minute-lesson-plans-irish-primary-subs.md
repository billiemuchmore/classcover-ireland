**The short answer:** When the lesson folder is empty, you already have everything you need. A small go-bag, a five-minute settling routine, a handful of universal activities by class level, and one of six free Irish resources will see any primary class through a day. The note you leave at home time decides whether the school books you again.

## The go-bag mindset

A tote bag, packed once, makes the difference between scrambling and starting strong. Five items, every booking:

- A read-aloud picture book and a chapter book. One for infants, one for senior classes. A good story holds any room for twenty minutes.
- A pad of plain A4 and a few coloured markers. Useful for everything from word walls to last-minute name tags.
- A small bundle of stickers or reward stamps. Not bribery. Recognition. Irish primary children respond to it.
- Your own pen, whiteboard marker and a USB stick. Schools rarely have spares to hand.
- A printed pack of three universal lessons. We come to those below.

If you are subbing across counties, keep a second copy of the pack in the car. You will not always know which school you are walking into until the morning.

## The first five minutes

Settling the class buys you the minutes you need to read whatever plan or note has been left. Three moves:

1. Write your name on the board before the children come in. Add today's date and one thing they will do today. *Today we are going to write a story together.* It signals competence.
2. As they sit, learn three names. Aim for the loudest, the quietest, and the child sitting closest to the door. You will use those three names all day.
3. Set the rhythm. Say what is happening for the first hour, when break is, and what you expect at the door. Children settle when they know the shape of the day.

This is the difference between a class that decides you are theirs and a class that decides you are not.

## Ready-to-run lessons by class level

These work in any English-medium Irish primary classroom. Each one ties to a curriculum area in the [Primary Curriculum Framework from the NCCA](https://ncca.ie/en/updates-and-events/latest-news/2023/may/the-primary-curriculum-framework/), so they slot into the school's plan without friction.

### Junior and Senior Infants (60 mins)

- 15 mins: a sing-along welcome with three action songs (use RTÉ Learn, below).
- 20 mins: read-aloud picture book, then a class wonder wall. *What did you wonder about that story?* Children take turns. You write each one on the board.
- 25 mins: free play stations with materials in the room. One quiet table, one moving table.

Curriculum area: Language, Arts Education, Wellbeing.

### 1st and 2nd Class (60 mins)

- 10 mins: a sketch starter. *Draw the room your story will happen in.*
- 20 mins: shared writing. Children call out the story. You write the first three lines on the board. They finish the page in their copies.
- 30 mins: a counting circle, then a maths game with whatever is on the wall. Most rooms have a number line and a hundred square.

Curriculum area: Language, STEM, Arts Education.

### 3rd and 4th Class (90 mins)

- 15 mins: silent reading from whatever is in their school bags. You read too.
- 30 mins: a school survey. Each child designs one question, collects five answers from classmates, then tallies the results into a bar chart in their copy.
- 45 mins: history or geography on Scoilnet. Pick one short video, then a class discussion, then a poster in groups of four.

Curriculum area: STEM, Social and Environmental Education, Language.

### 5th and 6th Class (90 mins)

- 20 mins: a *design a classroom for 2050* sketching task. Quiet. Independent.
- 30 mins: a paired research challenge on Scoilnet. Pairs pick a topic on the wall and find three facts they can present in two sentences.
- 40 mins: a class debate or small group presentations. Senior classes settle into formal talk well when you give them the structure.

Curriculum area: STEM, Wellbeing, Language, Arts Education.

## Six free resources every Irish primary sub should bookmark

Bookmark these once. You will use them every week.

- **[Scoilnet](https://www.scoilnet.ie)** is the Department of Education's official portal, with over 20,000 curriculum-mapped resources searchable by class level and subject. First place to look.
- **[RTÉ Learn](https://www.rte.ie/learn/)** has free primary videos, songs, science experiments and the full Home School Hub archive. Strong for infants and lower classes.
- **[COGG](https://cogg.ie)** is the state agency for Irish-medium education. Free Gaeilge teaching materials and posters that are useful even in English-medium schools.
- **[Cultúr Club from Conradh na Gaeilge](https://cnag.ie/en/get-involved/cultur-club/what-is-cult%C3%BAr-club.html)** has free activity books for 6 to 16-year-olds built around Irish identity and culture. Brilliant for Seachtain na Gaeilge and any cultural session.
- **[Oide Technology in Education](https://www.oidetechnologyineducation.ie)** runs free online courses, an AI Hub for teachers, and the Switched On digital citizenship programme for primary pupils.
- **[Twinkl Ireland](https://www.twinkl.ie/resources/roi-resources)** and **[Khan Academy Kids](https://www.khanacademy.org/kids)** round out the toolkit. Twinkl is freemium but its Republic of Ireland primary section is well-mapped. Khan Academy Kids is genuinely free and excellent for ages 2 to 8.

## Three wet-day Friday plans that need no resources

Print these. Carry them in the go-bag. They work in any room with a board and a few pencils.

### 1. The class storytelling circle

Children sit in a circle. You start a story with two sentences on the board. *There was a knock at the school door. Nobody else heard it.* Each child adds one sentence in turn. The story ends when the last child speaks. A volunteer reads the whole thing aloud at the end. Works from 1st Class up. Plan for thirty minutes.

### 2. The school survey

Each child writes one question they want answered by the rest of the class. You approve them. Then everyone walks around the room collecting answers from five other children. Back at desks, each child draws a small bar chart of their results. The class shares the most surprising finding. Twenty to forty minutes depending on level. Hits maths, language and a little geography.

### 3. The classroom of the future

Hand out plain A4. The brief: design the classroom you would build in 2050. Children sketch on one side and write three sentences on the other explaining their three favourite features. The best ones go on the board. Twenty minutes minimum, an hour if you give them more time. Hits Arts Education, Language and Wellbeing.

## The handover note that gets you re-booked

The last ten minutes of the day decide whether the school books you again. Leave a short note on the regular teacher's desk. Four lines.

1. What the class actually did. The two or three activities they completed, in order.
2. Any child who needed an extra hand, and what helped.
3. One small win you noticed. Names matter here.
4. Your name, your number, and one line on what days you have free in the coming weeks.

This note is the single highest-leverage thing you do all day. A principal who sees it on Monday morning will think of you the next time a teacher calls in.
